Detailed comments to authors, including aspects that must be improved in the camera-ready version of the
paper:
The paper addresses a relevant problem in modern identity and access management infrastructure, proposing a
self−hosted, high−availability architecture as an alternative to vendor−managed solutions such as
Keycloak. While the motivation regarding vendor lock−in is understandable, the authors’ critique of
Keycloak’s reliance on Amazon Aurora PostgreSQL appears somewhat inconsistent, as Aurora is itself a
managed PostgreSQL service that does not fundamentally differ from other cloud−hosted database offerings.
This observation weakens the initial problem statement and would benefit from clearer articulation of what
specific aspects of vendor lock−in the authors intend to address.
Regarding the architectural approach, the paper criticizes a Kubernetes−based deployment strategy and
characterizes it as introducing unnecessary complexity for fixed−capacity deployments. This claim is
debatable, as Kubernetes has become a de facto standard for container orchestration and offers benefits
beyond mere scalability, including automated recovery, declarative configuration , and ecosystem maturity.
The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of when Kubernetes−based approaches are and are
not appropriate.
The authors present a custom system for PostgreSQL scaling that combines etcd for leader election with in−
database status tracking to maintain consistency among peers. While this approach is technically sound, it

essentially replicates functionality that is already available in container orchestration platforms such as
Kubernetes, raising questions about the motivation. A clearer justification for building a custom
solution rather than leveraging existing platform capabilities would strengthen the paper’s contribution.
From a completeness standpoint, several aspects of the system are not fully explained. Some acronyms,
particularly SNI and VIP management, appear without definition, which may hinder readers unfamiliar with
these networking concepts. Additionally, the third node in the test bed, described as the backend, lacks
sufficient detail for reproducibility . The authors also propose standard observability patterns using the
Grafana stack with Prometheus and Loki, which is reasonable, but the omission of distributed tracing
represents a missed opportunity for comprehensive system observability.
The deployment approach using Docker Compose is presented as enabling atomic updates and version rollback.
While containers do facilitate deployment reproducibility, the claim regarding atomicity is not entirely
accurate, as Docker Compose does not provide true atomic rolling updates without additional tooling or
orchestration. Furthermore, the authors do not consider Docker Swarm as a clustering solution, instead
developing their own clustering mechanism. The reported deployment time of 8−10 minutes, while
functional, is notably longer than what contemporary infrastructure−as−code and GitOps approaches can
achieve.
The experimental evaluation presents several limitations that affect the paper’s impact. The authors test only
their proposed system without comparison against alternative solutions under identical conditions, which
limits the ability to assess the relative merits of the approach. The scale in Figure 2 appears to warrant
a logarithmic presentation for clarity . Additionally, the comparison in Table 3 is unfair to the baseline ,
as the self −hosted solution does not account for multi−availability zone deployments, which is a critical
consideration for production−grade high−availability systems. Finally, the paper would benefit from
following academic conventions regarding reference formatting, as links to documentation pages should
typically appear as footnotes rather than in the reference list .

Detailed comments to authors, including aspects that must be improved in the camera-ready version of the
paper:
The paper clearly addresses a pragmatic and relevant challenge: deploying a self −hosted Identity and Access
Management (IAM) solution with High Availability (HA). The paper references Aurora PostgreSQL and
focuses on PostgreSQL in the implementation. However, Keycloak officially supports multiple database
systems (e.g., MariaDB, MySQL, MSSQL and Oracle). The rationale for selecting PostgreSQL as the
exclusive database layer is not sufficiently justified. At the same time, the framing could further emphasise
the academic novelty and methodological contribution besides the engineering integration.
In particular , the following improvements are recommended:
* Clarify more explicitly what is novel compared to existing Keycloak HA approaches and reference
architectures.
*
Strengthen the problem statement by distinguishing amongst the engineering integration, the empirical
validation and the methodological reproducibility.
* Explicitly articulate the research contribution in methodological terms.
* With regard to the selected technical components, briefly explain why alternatives were excluded.
*
Increase the emphasis on peer−reviewed academic literature (e.g. HA architectures for stateful services ,
failover detection mechanisms and IAM performance benchmarking), as the bibliography relies heavily on
product documentation and manuals.

* Explicitly state cost modelling assumptions, as the cost comparison is useful but lacks detailed assumptions

* Acknowledge that the cloud provider comparison is subject to limitations (or consider additional providers),

Detailed comments to authors, including aspects that must be improved in the camera-ready version of the
paper:
The paper demonstrates a reliable, self −hosted, high−availability Keycloak setup that achieves strong
performance and seamless failover for mid−scale enterpriseswithout needing Kubernetes or cloud−specific
infrastructure .
While the experiments follow a rigorous statistical design, additional results would significantly strengthen
the overall validation . Firstly , more trials (higher n) for both performance and failover scenarios would
help demonstrate production durability, not just short benchmark success. Secondly, authentication systems
behave differently depending on configuration and use case. Broader, mixed workloads would show the
architecture works beyond the specific tested case. Finally, the failover test is strong (0% error during
recovery), but only one failure class appears tested. More convincing validation would include network
partitions, node crashes vs. graceful shutdown, ansd multi−component cascading failures.
